## What Is Mutual Divorce in India?

Mutual divorce, also called divorce by mutual consent, is a legal process where both husband and wife jointly agree to end their marriage peacefully. In India, this process is mainly governed by Section 13B of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955.

Unlike contested divorce cases, mutual divorce does not require one spouse to prove cruelty, adultery, or harassment. Both parties agree that the marriage has broken down and cannot continue.

## Eligibility for Mutual Consent Divorce

To file a mutual divorce petition in India, couples must satisfy certain conditions:

- Husband and wife should have lived separately for at least one year
- Both spouses must mutually agree for divorce
- There should be no force, fraud, or pressure involved
- Both parties should settle matters related to:
  - Alimony or maintenance
  - Child custody
  - Property division
  - Financial settlement

Indian courts generally encourage settlement and peaceful separation in mutual divorce matters.

### Step 2: Prepare the Settlement Agreement

Before filing the divorce petition, both spouses prepare a mutual settlement agreement. This document usually includes:

- Alimony amount
- Child custody arrangements
- Property and asset distribution
- Loan responsibilities
- Withdrawal of pending legal complaints

This agreement becomes one of the most important documents in the mutual divorce process.

### Step 3: File the First Motion Petition

The joint divorce petition is filed before the Family Court having jurisdiction.

In Tamil Nadu, couples generally file in:

- Chennai Family Court
- Coimbatore Family Court
- Court where marriage took place
- Court where spouses last lived together

The petition contains:

- Marriage details
- Separation period
- Reason for divorce
- Mutual consent declaration

### Step 4: First Court Hearing

Both spouses must appear before the Family Court judge. The court verifies:

- Identity of both parties
- Genuine mutual consent
- Settlement agreement
- Voluntary decision

The judge may attempt reconciliation before proceeding further.

### Step 5: Cooling-Off Period

After the first motion, Indian law generally provides a six-month cooling-off period to allow reconciliation between spouses.

However, the Supreme Court has allowed waiver of this period in appropriate cases if:

- There is no chance of reconciliation
- Settlement issues are already resolved
- Parties have been separated for a long time

Courts in Chennai and Coimbatore may waive the cooling period depending on the facts of the case.

### Step 6: Second Motion Hearing

After the waiting period or waiver approval, the second motion is filed.

During this stage:

- Both spouses confirm their final decision
- Court verifies settlement compliance
- Final statements are recorded

If everything is satisfactory, the Family Court grants the divorce decree.

### Step 7: Final Divorce Decree

Once approved, the court issues the mutual consent divorce decree. This legally dissolves the marriage.

After the decree:

- Both parties are legally separated
- Settlement terms become enforceable
- Either spouse can remarry legally
